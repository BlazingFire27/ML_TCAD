A Comprehensive Research Plan for SciML-Driven Surrogate Modeling of 1D Silicon OxidationIntroduction to Semiconductor Surrogate Modeling and Digital TwinsThe thermal oxidation of silicon represents one of the most critical foundational processes in semiconductor device fabrication, providing the necessary dielectric isolation, surface passivation, and gate oxide layers required for modern complementary metal-oxide-semiconductor (CMOS) technology. As the microelectronics industry aggressively pursues nanometer-scale scaling and advanced three-dimensional device architectures, the precise control of the silicon dioxide ($SiO_2$) growth kinetics becomes increasingly paramount. Traditionally, the prediction of oxide thickness, dopant redistribution, and interface evolution relies heavily on Technology Computer-Aided Design (TCAD) simulations based on high-fidelity numerical techniques such as the finite element method (FEM) or the finite volume method (FVM). While these traditional numerical solvers offer high degrees of physical accuracy, they suffer from severe computational bottlenecks, particularly when deployed in iterative optimization loops, inverse design problems, or real-time process control scenarios where thousands of sequential evaluations are required.To circumvent the immense computational expense of high-fidelity TCAD simulations, the development of digital twins and surrogate models has become a focal point of computational microelectronics and semiconductor manufacturing research. A digital twin in this context is not merely a static representation or a simple lookup table, but rather an evolving, dynamic virtual replica of the physical oxidation process capable of rapid inference and complex scenario analysis. Recently, Scientific Machine Learning (SciML) has emerged as a transformative paradigm to construct these surrogate models, bridging the gap between purely empirical data-driven approaches and deterministic physical models. Unlike traditional black-box neural networks, which struggle with out-of-distribution generalization and frequently violate underlying physical laws such as mass conservation or thermodynamic equilibrium, physics-informed architectures integrate the governing partial differential equations (PDEs) directly into the learning mechanism.Constructing a highly accurate digital twin for a one-dimensional silicon oxidation process presents a unique confluence of multiphysics and mathematical challenges. The process is governed by coupled reaction-diffusion dynamics that exhibit extremely stiff concentration gradients, particularly during the initial phases of oxide growth. Furthermore, the physical domain is not static; the continuous consumption of the silicon substrate and the resulting volumetric expansion of the oxide layer create a moving boundary problem, classically known in applied mathematics as a Stefan problem. Accommodating the robust and time-tested Deal-Grove analytical framework, managing complex atmospheric boundary conditions dictated by fluctuating gas partial pressures, handling the geometrical complexities of volume expansion, and addressing the data-scarcity inherent in TCAD calibration require a highly sophisticated, multi-faceted research approach. This report delineates an exhaustive, end-to-end research plan to construct a high-fidelity surrogate model for one-dimensional silicon oxidation utilizing Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) and advanced operator learning frameworks, specifically Deep Operator Networks (DeepONets) and Fourier Neural Operators (FNOs).Physical Formulation and the Deal-Grove FrameworkThe bedrock of any physics-informed surrogate model is the accurate mathematical representation of the underlying phenomenological behavior. For the thermal oxidation of planar silicon surfaces, the Deal-Grove model, first postulated by Bruce Deal and Andrew Grove in 1965, remains the quintessential theoretical framework upon which decades of semiconductor manufacturing have been based. The model conceptualizes the macroscopic oxidation process through three sequential fluxes occurring in a state of dynamic steady-state equilibrium. Translating this physical model into a format suitable for a neural network loss function requires a rigorous definition of these fluxes and their interplay.Flux Balances and Reaction-Diffusion DynamicsThe Deal-Grove model inherently assumes that the oxidation reaction occurs exclusively at the internal interface between the growing oxide layer and the silicon substrate, rather than at the external surface exposed to the ambient gas. This necessitates the transport of the oxidizing species, typically either dry oxygen ($O_2$) or water vapor ($H_2O$), from the ambient atmosphere through the existing, continuously thickening oxide layer. The entire kinetic process is governed by three distinct, sequential fluxes.The first flux, denoted as the gas-phase transport flux ($F_1$), represents the transport of the oxidant from the bulk of the ambient gas to the outer surface of the oxide layer. This flux is mathematically expressed as $F_1 = h_G(C_G - C_S)$, where $h_G$ represents the gas-phase mass-transfer coefficient, $C_G$ is the concentration of the oxidant in the bulk gas, and $C_S$ is the oxidant concentration immediately adjacent to the physical oxide surface. Utilizing the ideal gas law, which defines concentration as $C = P/kT$, this mass-transfer flux can be rewritten in terms of partial pressures as $F_1 = (h_G/kT)(P_G - P_S)$. In most practical furnace operations, the gas flow rate is sufficiently high that $h_G$ is massive, making this first step rarely the rate-limiting factor in the overall oxidation process.The second flux, denoted as the oxide diffusion flux ($F_2$), captures the diffusion of the oxidant through the existing $SiO_2$ layer towards the underlying silicon substrate. Governed by Fick's First Law of diffusion, this flux is expressed as $F_2 = -D \frac{\partial C}{\partial x}$, where $D$ is the effective diffusion coefficient of the particular oxidant within the amorphous silicon dioxide network. Assuming a steady-state condition where the concentration gradient remains approximately linear across the entirety of the oxide thickness $x_{ox}$, the flux simplifies algebraically to $F_2 = D(C_0 - C_I)/x_{ox}$, with $C_0$ and $C_I$ representing the oxidant concentrations at the outer oxide surface and the internal $Si-SiO_2$ interface, respectively.The third and final flux, denoted as the interface reaction flux ($F_3$), represents the actual chemical consumption of the silicon at the substrate surface. This is assumed to be a first-order chemical reaction with respect to the oxidant concentration available at the interface. This flux is defined as $F_3 = k_s C_I$, where $k_s$ is the effective surface reaction rate constant, encompassing complex sub-processes such as bond-breaking and molecular rearrangement.Under the fundamental assumption of steady-state kinetics, these three sequential fluxes must be entirely equal at all times, such that $F_1 = F_2 = F_3$. Equating these fluxes allows for the mathematical elimination of the unknown intermediate concentrations ($C_0$ and $C_I$), yielding a single governing differential equation for the rate of oxide growth:
$$\frac{dx_{ox}}{dt} = \frac{B}{A + 2x_{ox}}$$Integrating this differential equation with respect to time, applying the initial condition $x_{ox}(t=0) = x_i$ to account for any pre-existing native oxide on the wafer surface, yields the standard, universally recognized Deal-Grove relationship:$$x_{ox}^2 + A x_{ox} = B(t + \tau)$$
Within this framework, the parameter $B$ is identified as the parabolic rate constant, which dictates the diffusion-limited growth regime predominantly observed for thicker oxides. The quotient $B/A$ is identified as the linear rate constant, which governs the reaction-limited growth regime dominant during the initial stages of oxidation on a bare or nearly bare silicon surface. Finally, $\tau$ represents a time-shift parameter that mathematically accounts for the presence of the initial oxide layer, effectively synchronizing the theoretical growth curve with the physical starting conditions.To build a comprehensive PINN, the neural network must be constrained by the localized differential form of Fick's Second Law ($\frac{\partial C}{\partial t} = D \frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial x^2}$) for the interior of the oxide, while the boundaries are strictly governed by the flux balance equations derived from the Deal-Grove model.Thermodynamics of Partial Pressures and Ambient Flow RatesTo construct a highly generalized and practically useful surrogate model, the boundary conditions at the external gas-oxide interface must dynamically reflect the true thermodynamic state of the oxidation furnace. According to Henry's Law, the equilibrium concentration of the oxidant in the solid oxide surface ($C^*$) is directly and linearly proportional to the partial pressure of the oxidant gas ($P_G$) in the surrounding ambient atmosphere. This fundamental thermodynamic relationship is expressed as $C^* = H \cdot P_G$, where $H$ is the specific Henry's Law constant for the gas-solid system at a given temperature.In typical semiconductor manufacturing processes, the oxidizing ambient is rarely a pure gas. It is often a precisely metered mixture of reactive gases, such as pure oxygen, and inert diluent gases, such as nitrogen or argon, which are used to meticulously control and retard the growth rate of ultra-thin oxides required for modern gate dielectrics. The partial pressure of oxygen ($P_{O_2}$) within this mixture is a critical determinant of both the linear and parabolic rate constants in the Deal-Grove formulation. According to Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures, the total absolute pressure of an ideal gas mixture is equal to the sum of the individual partial pressures of its constituent gases ($P_{total} = \sum P_i$). The partial pressure of any specific gas is equivalent to its mole fraction multiplied by the total chamber pressure. Assuming ideal gas behavior where volumetric flow rates directly correspond to molar flow rates, the partial pressure of oxygen in a continuous flow oxidation furnace can be calculated directly from the mass flow controllers. These controllers dictate the flow rates, typically measured in Standard Cubic Centimeters per Minute (SCCM), of the constituent gases. The relationship is formalized as:
$$P_{O_2} = \left( \frac{Q_{O_2}}{Q_{O_2} + Q_{N_2}} \right) P_{total}$$
where $Q_{O_2}$ and $Q_{N_2}$ represent the respective volumetric flow rates in SCCM.The original Deal-Grove model indicates that the parabolic rate constant $B$ is linearly proportional to the ambient partial pressure ($B \propto P_{O_2}$) over all oxide thickness ranges, because the diffusion driving force is directly tied to the maximum oxidant solubility limit $C^*$ at the surface. The linear rate constant $B/A$ also exhibits a distinct pressure dependence, although experimental evidence reveals that empirical deviations occur for ultra-thin regimes below 300 Angstroms, where the pressure dependence can shift from a strictly linear relationship to a fractional power law. By mathematically embedding the SCCM-to-partial-pressure conversion algorithm directly into the PINN's Dirichlet boundary constraints, the surrogate model gains the unprecedented capacity to predict transient oxide thickness across a continuous spectrum of arbitrary furnace gas flow configurations. This direct linkage between macroscopic machine inputs (SCCM) and microscopic physical models is a vital requirement for the deployment of a real-world digital twin on the fabrication floor.The Moving Boundary Problem and Spatial RegularizationA primary mathematical complexity in modeling the thermal oxidation of silicon is the tracking and handling of the $Si-SiO_2$ interface, which continuously propagates downward into the silicon substrate over time. This dynamic behavior is a classic manifestation of the Stefan problem, a specific class of boundary value problems for partial differential equations where the phase boundary moves as a direct function of the underlying field gradients evaluated at that boundary. In the specific context of silicon oxidation, the velocity of the interface is directly coupled to the diffusion flux of oxygen arriving at the interface and the subsequent chemical reaction rate.Furthermore, the conversion of the crystalline silicon lattice to an amorphous silicon dioxide network involves a drastic change in molecular density and volume. The density of solid silicon is approximately $2.33 \text{ g/cm}^3$, while the density of amorphous $SiO_2$ is lower, at approximately $2.21 \text{ g/cm}^3$. Accounting for the molecular weights and densities, the volume of the resulting $SiO_2$ is significantly larger than the volume of the original silicon consumed. Specifically, established physical models dictate that for an oxide layer of total thickness $d$ grown, a layer of silicon corresponding to a thickness of exactly $0.44d$ is consumed. Because the overall volume expands by approximately 127%, the external free surface of the $SiO_2$ does not remain coplanar with the original, pre-oxidation silicon surface. Instead, the oxide physically swells outward into the ambient gas.If the original, pristine silicon surface is designated mathematically as the spatial origin ($x = 0$), the growth of an oxide layer possessing a total thickness of $X_{ox}$ means the new, buried $Si-SiO_2$ interface is located at the positive coordinate $x = 0.44 X_{ox}$. Simultaneously, the free gas-oxide surface expands in the negative spatial direction, reaching a coordinate of $x = -0.56 X_{ox}$. Handling these continuously evolving negative spatial coordinates and the temporally expanding physical domain $[ -0.56 X_{ox}(t), 0.44 X_{ox}(t) ]$ within a fixed computational grid or standard Eulerian mesh is tremendously frustrating and computationally expensive. In standard finite element methods, such moving boundary problems require continuous dynamic remeshing, the implementation of complex Arbitrary Lagrangian-Eulerian (ALE) formulations, or the use of diffuse-interface phase-field methods to track the sharp interface, all of which incur heavy computational penalties and stability issues. To build a fast, mesh-free SciML surrogate using neural networks, the spatial domain must be mathematically regularized before being passed to the input layer.Coordinate Transformation and Domain NormalizationTo train a physics-informed neural network effectively and stably on a domain characterized by a moving boundary, the transient physical domain must be mapped algebraically to a fixed, normalized computational domain. This crucial step ensures that the internal collocation points used for calculating the continuous PDE residuals in the PINN remain uniformly distributed over the training space and do not require complex, dynamic resampling algorithms at every simulated time step.The regularization strategy relies on a continuous coordinate transformation, mapping the physical coordinates $x_{phys}(t) \in [-0.56 X_{ox}(t), 0.44 X_{ox}(t)]$ into a stationary, normalized reference domain denoted by $\xi \in $. The transformation function mapping the physical space to the latent space is defined as:
$$\xi(x_{phys}, t) = \frac{x_{phys} + 0.56 X_{ox}(t)}{X_{ox}(t)}$$
Consequently, the coordinate $\xi = 0$ permanently corresponds to the external gas-oxide surface regardless of its actual physical expansion, and the coordinate $\xi = 1$ permanently corresponds to the propagating, internal $Si-SiO_2$ interface. By utilizing this dynamic mapping, the time-dependent geometry is effectively "frozen" within the neural network's reference domain, eliminating the complexities of negative spatial growth.However, mapping the physical domain fundamentally alters the governing reaction-diffusion PDEs. By applying the chain rule of differentiation, the spatial and temporal derivatives in the original Fickian diffusion equation must be augmented by a deformation gradient tensor. The transformation introduces apparent convective-like advection terms into the standard diffusion equation. For instance, the temporal derivative of the oxidant concentration field $C(x_{phys},t)$ transforms as:
$$\left. \frac{\partial C}{\partial t} \right|_{x_{phys}} = \left. \frac{\partial C}{\partial t} \right|_\xi + \frac{\partial C}{\partial \xi} \frac{\partial \xi}{\partial t}$$where $\frac{\partial \xi}{\partial t}$ represents the effective mesh velocity induced by the continuous volumetric expansion. Similarly, the spatial derivatives undergo a scaling transformation dictated by the inverse of the total oxide thickness:$$\frac{\partial C}{\partial x_{phys}} = \frac{1}{X_{ox}(t)} \frac{\partial C}{\partial \xi}$$$$\frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial x_{phys}^2} = \frac{1}{X_{ox}^2(t)} \frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial \xi^2}$$These fully transformed, coordinate-mapped PDEs are then hard-coded directly into the residual loss formulation of the PINN framework. This elegant mathematical maneuver entirely removes the necessity for dynamic remeshing, drastically reducing the complexity of the neural network's learning landscape, guaranteeing spatial generalization, and totally circumventing the mathematical complications of representing negative spatial growth coordinates.Dataset Generation via Latin Hypercube SamplingWhile the foundational premise of physics-informed neural networks is to minimize the strict reliance on massive datasets by embedding known differential equations, practical surrogate modeling for complex semiconductor manufacturing processes still requires data. Initial boundary conditions, highly specialized transport coefficients, and material property variations for novel, ultra-thin regimes or heavily doped substrates often require rigorous calibration against high-fidelity TCAD data before the neural network can accurately generalize. The generation of this foundational, ground-truth dataset represents a critical, computationally intensive phase of the research plan. Because commercial semiconductor TCAD simulation suites (such as Synopsys Sentaurus or Silvaco Atlas) are immensely computationally expensive—sometimes requiring several hours per simulation to resolve coupled multiphysics processes on refined meshes—naive random Monte Carlo sampling of the multi-dimensional parameter space is vastly inefficient and impractical.To construct an optimal, highly representative training dataset with minimal computational expenditure, Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS) must be deployed as the primary Design of Experiments (DoE) methodology. LHS is an advanced stratified sampling technique that ensures a near-uniform, exhaustive exploration of the multidimensional design space without the clustering and gaps associated with pseudo-random number generation. By mathematically dividing the cumulative distribution function of each independent parameter into $N$ equally probable intervals, and subsequently selecting exactly one sample randomly from each interval, LHS guarantees that both the extreme margins and the dense bulk of the parameter space are evaluated. This ensures that the neural network encounters a highly diverse set of physical states during its training phase.For the development of the silicon oxidation surrogate, the LHS design space will be A Physics-Informed Neural Network fundamentally alters the paradigm of machine learning by embedding the governing differential equations, boundary conditions, and physical constraints directly into the backpropagation loop, compelling the network to respect the inviolable laws of thermodynamics and mass conservation.Network Architecture and Composite Loss FunctionThe foundational PINN architecture for the 1D silicon oxidation problem relies on a deep, fully connected feed-forward neural network. The inputs to the network will be the mapped, stationary spatial coordinate ($\xi$) and time ($t$), concatenated with the macroscopic process parameters (Temperature $T$ and Partial Pressure $P_{O_2}$). The primary outputs generated by the network will be the oxidant concentration field across the normalized domain $C(\xi, t)$ and the predicted macroscopic oxide thickness $X_{ox}(t)$.The total loss function ($\mathcal{L}_{total}$) optimized during the gradient descent training phase is a complex, weighted composite of several distinct physical and empirical penalty terms :
$$\mathcal{L}_{total} = w_{data}\mathcal{L}_{data} + w_{PDE}\mathcal{L}_{PDE} + w_{IC}\mathcal{L}_{IC} + w_{BC}\mathcal{L}_{BC} + w_{Stefan}\mathcal{L}_{Stefan}$$The individual components of this composite loss function are defined as follows:Loss ComponentMathematical/Physical DescriptionPurpose within the PINNData Loss ($\mathcal{L}_{data}$)Computes the Mean Squared Error (MSE) between the neural network's predictions and the sparse LHS-generated TCAD dataset.Anchors the model to reality, calibrating unknown or highly variable physical coefficients.PDE Residual Loss ($\mathcal{L}_{PDE}$)Evaluated at a dense set of unsupervised collocation points scattered throughout the $(\xi, t)$ domain. It couples the time derivative of the oxide thickness ($\frac{dX_{ox}}{dt}$) to the local spatial derivative of the concentration field ($\frac{\partial C}{\partial \xi}$).Guarantees that the predicted boundary velocity strictly obeys the macroscopic conservation of mass, tying the consumption of silicon directly to the arrival of oxygen atoms.Addressing Reaction-Diffusion Stiffness: NTK and Adaptive WeightingA pervasive and well-documented pathology in training PINNs on reaction-diffusion equations is the inherent mathematical stiffness of the system. Thermal oxidation kinetics feature a boundary layer with extreme, highly localized concentration gradients, particularly during the rapid, initial reaction-limited growth phase where oxygen is consumed almost instantaneously upon reaching the silicon interface. During the backpropagation of the neural network, the gradients of the various loss components (for example, the boundary condition loss governing the surface pressure versus the PDE residual loss governing the slow internal diffusion) can exhibit wildly divergent magnitudes and conflicting directions. This phenomenon leads to severe spectral bias and gradient flow pathologies. Consequently, the network often converges prematurely on the smooth, low-frequency macroscopic dynamics (the general parabolic curve) while entirely failing to resolve the sharp, high-frequency transients and the stiff concentration gradients at the moving $Si-SiO_2$ interface.To rectify this fundamental limitation, the research plan incorporates an advanced adaptive loss weighting strategy strictly predicated on Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) theory. The NTK is a theoretical framework that perfectly characterizes the dynamic evolution of a neural network during gradient descent training in the limit of infinite network width. By continuously evaluating the eigenvalues of the NTK matrices associated with each individual loss term, the convergence rates of the disparate physical constraints can be monitored and dynamically balanced.Specifically, if the mathematical trace of the NTK matrix associated with the boundary condition loss becomes significantly larger than the trace for the PDE residual loss, the network will disproportionately over-optimize the boundaries at the severe expense of the interior domain physics. The NTK-guided adaptive weighting algorithm actively mitigates this by continuously updating the scalar weights ($w_{PDE}, w_{BC}, w_{Stefan}$) at each epoch by normalizing them against the trace of their respective NTK matrices. This rigorous mathematical normalization guarantees that no single loss component dominates the gradient flow, ensuring uniform, harmonious convergence across the entire spatiotemporal domain and maintaining strict structural integrity at the critically important, highly stiff $Si-SiO_2$ interface.Additionally, to prevent non-physical predictions such as negative oxidant concentrations or negative oxide thicknesses during the chaotic early training epochs, a soft-clamping head or specific activation function tailoring can be introduced to the network output, strictly enforcing an admissible physical state space of $C \geq 0$ and $X_{ox} \geq 0$.Operator Learning: Generalization via DeepONets and FNOsWhile a highly optimized, NTK-weighted standard PINN is exceptionally effective at interpolating within the specific confines of its trained parameters, it fundamentally acts as a single-instance solver. If a boundary condition changes continuously—such as a complex, dynamic flow rate profile where $O_2$ and $N_2$ SCCM values are modulated mid-process by a controller—the standard PINN must generally be retrained or extensively parameterized to handle the new state space. To achieve a true, real-time digital twin capable of immediate zero-shot inference across an infinite-dimensional functional space, the research plan dictates a transition from instance-specific PINNs to advanced Operator Learning frameworks, specifically Deep Operator Networks (DeepONets) and Fourier Neural Operators (FNOs).Unlike standard neural networks that map a discrete set of input vectors to output vectors, operator learning algorithms map entire infinite-dimensional functional spaces to other functional spaces, thereby learning the underlying continuous PDE operator itself rather than a discrete, point-wise solution field.Deep Operator Networks (DeepONets)DeepONets are firmly grounded in the universal approximation theorem for operators, proving their capacity to learn any continuous non-linear operator mapping. The unique architecture bifurcates the learning process into two distinct, parallel neural pathways:The Branch Network: This network is responsible for encoding the input function. In the specific context of the silicon oxidation digital twin, the input functions are the time-varying partial pressure profiles $P_{O_2}(t)$ derived from varying gas flow rates, or the spatially varying initial dopant concentration profiles in the silicon substrate. The branch network samples these continuous functions at a set of discrete "sensor" locations and extracts a rich, latent feature vector representing the overall functional shape.The Trunk Network: This network encodes the continuous spatiotemporal target coordinates $(\xi, t)$ where the specific solution evaluation is desired. It acts as the spatial basis for the operator.The final system output—the continuous oxidant concentration field and the exact location of the moving interface—is computed via a simple inner dot product of the outputs generated by the branch and trunk networks. Because the branch network is designed to process temporal sequences and functional data, the integration of a Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) or Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) module within the branch architecture will allow the DeepONet to efficiently capture historical time dependencies. This makes the architecture exceptionally well-suited for dynamic furnace processes where gas flows are frequently modulated in real-time to achieve specific oxide targets. Once comprehensively trained on the LHS dataset, the DeepONet will serve as a mesh-free, ultra-fast surrogate, evaluating the full, complex oxidation state in fractions of a millisecond for any newly inputted, unseen boundary condition.Fourier Neural Operators (FNOs)As a robust parallel pathway, the research will rigorously evaluate the efficacy of Fourier Neural Operators (FNOs) for the same task. FNOs approach operator learning through a fundamentally different mathematical lens, transforming the input functions and spatial data into the frequency domain via the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT). The architecture replaces standard linear layers with spectral convolution layers that filter out high-frequency noise and learn the integral kernel of the governing PDE directly in Fourier space before applying an inverse FFT to return to the physical domain.FNOs exhibit remarkable computational efficiency and are inherently resolution-invariant. This unique property means that an FNO can be trained entirely on the highly coarse grid data generated by the accelerated LHS-TCAD simulations to save compute time, and subsequently deployed during inference to predict fields on highly refined, dense meshes without any loss of accuracy, interpolation errors, or the need for retraining.Comparative Assessment for the Moving Boundary ProblemA critical deliverable of this research plan involves benchmarking the DeepONet architecture against the FNO architecture specifically for the 1D moving boundary oxidation problem.
